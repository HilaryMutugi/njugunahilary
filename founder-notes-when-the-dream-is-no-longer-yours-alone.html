When the Dream Is No Longer Yours Alone

Njuguna HilaryFounder, AfrifamaJune 18, 2026

For a long time, the dream was almost entirely mine to hold.

I was the one explaining it to people who were not sure whether to believe me or feel sorry for me. The one showing up when the energy was gone, the account was thin, and the results were taking longer than I had planned.

The one keeping the whole thing alive on a combination of discipline, prayer, and a level of optimism that probably had no business existing given the circumstances.

I wrote about that season a lot. And I meant every word.

But something has shifted lately, and I think it is worth writing about honestly.

Somewhere in these past months, I have watched something happen that I did not fully expect. I have seen a team of young people — genuinely incredible people — start to put their own lives on the line for what we are building at Afrifama.

Not because they had to. Not because there was some grand promise on the table. But because somewhere along the line, they believed enough to stay and carry a part of this thing with me.

And I will be honest. That does something to you.

Because for so long, the sacrifice was mine to carry and almost mine alone. You almost build an identity around being the person who holds everything together.

The one who refuses to let the vision die. The one who pushes through on vibes, prayer, and a level of optimism that probably had no business existing given the circumstances.

And then one day you look around and realise the weight is being shared. Not perfectly, not always the way you would do it yourself, but it is being shared. People are showing up to believe and are sacrificing in their own way for something that started as an idea in your head.

And that is both beautiful and a little terrifying, if I am being honest.

I do not use the title CEO very often. Maybe because when you are truly in the trenches, the title can feel slightly out of place. One minute you are discussing strategy, and the next you are asking why a delivery is delayed, why a cost changed overnight, why something that should have been simple has decided to become complicated. The title and the reality do not always feel like they belong to the same story.

But technically, I am. And lately, I have been understanding what that actually means, or at least what it is supposed to mean.

Being a founder at the start means being everywhere. You are the engine and the emergency department. The marketer and the person chasing invoices. The accountant staring at an Excel sheet that has absolutely no mercy. You trust your own hands because your own hands are often all you have.

But as the business grows, you cannot stay there forever. At some point, the work demands that you evolve. From doing everything to providing direction. From solving every problem yourself to building systems so that problems get solved without you being the only answer. From carrying the fire alone to learning how to pass it carefully, without burning everything in the process.

And that shift is not easy.

Doing everything feels safer. At least when it is in your hands, you know exactly how hard you are pushing. You know what corners are being cut and which ones are not. You know the madness that is keeping it alive.

But leadership demands that you start trusting other people too. That you allow them to grow into responsibility. That you correct without killing morale. That you push without breaking people. That you are honest about the pressure while still giving the team something to believe in.

And I think that is the part nobody prepares you for.

Going from the person on the frontline sacrificing everything, to the person whose job is to provide direction and curate the vision — if I am being honest, it feels both more meaningful and heavier at the same time.

More meaningful because the dream is no longer just mine. Other people can see it now. Other people feel it. They have chosen to show up for it. And that tells you that what you are building has moved beyond an idea. It has started to become something real.

Heavier because when you are leading people, your decisions travel further than just yourself. Your mistakes are no longer only personal. They touch morale, they touch trust, they touch the future you are asking everyone to believe in. When you were alone, you could absorb the consequences quietly. When you are leading a team, the consequences move through everyone.

And maybe that is why leadership feels different from sacrifice.

Sacrifice is about what you are willing to lose.
Leadership is about what you are responsible for protecting.

I will not pretend I have figured it all out. Some days, I still want to just be the guy on the frontline doing everything by force, because at least that version of the work is familiar. But I know that if Afrifama is going to grow into what I believe it can become, I have to grow too.

I have to learn to provide direction more clearly. To build systems that hold even when I am not in the room. To communicate better. To let people carry real responsibility. To lead, and not just hustle.

Somewhere between the founder who carried the dream alone and the leader learning to carry it together with a team — that is where I am right now. Not perfectly. Not magically. But honestly, one day at a time.

The dream is no longer mine alone. And maybe that is the scariest, most beautiful part of all of this.
